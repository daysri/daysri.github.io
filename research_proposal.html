A Multimodal Auto-grading System using Retrieval Augmented Generation Large Language Model

PhD Research Proposal

Irsyad Arif Mashudi
College Lecturer, Politeknik Negeri Malang

Research Background

In the rapidly changing education environment, educators and educational institutions face the challenge of evaluating student work more efficiently, accurately, and highly. This problem emanates from conventional grading systems, which consume a lot of time and effort. Such systems often lead to significant impediments in the feedback process, which is critical for performance improvement.

To address these obstacles, there has been a surge in automated grading solutions that utilize increasingly complex artificial intelligence (AI), especially natural language processing (NLP), to assess students works. However, these auto-grading tools usually rely heavily on text inputs, thus limiting their capability to capture the whole scope of student work.

The idea of multimodal learning has been coined to address this shortfall and suggests that students could convey their understanding through various means, such as images, text, speech, diagrams, and other media. Given this perspective, an auto-grading system should be able to evaluate diverse input data types to allow a more comprehensive evaluation.

Problem Statement

Traditional auto-grading systems often rely solely on the capability of the Large Language Model. This approach has many drawbacks. Because of its reliance on LLM, it may only be able to give a general response. This poses a problem when the system is used to evaluate some specific subject. The response generated may not be accurate or even outright wrong.

Another approach is to fine-tune the LLM to build the auto-grading system. The weakness of this approach is that we need to find an optimum hyperparameter for the LLM. Often, the found hyperparameter is only the local optimum because of the try-and-error approach.

Research Problems

Limited Scope of Existing Tools
Current AI-driven grading solutions primarily target text-based submissions, neglecting images, audio, and other non-text inputs.
Incomplete Assessment
The underrepresentation of multimodal work results in partial evaluations that fail to capture students' full range of knowledge and skills.
Static Data Dependencies
Many existing platforms rely on text-only or outdated data, making them difficult to update with new curricular materials.
Dynamic Adaptability
Such a system should dynamically incorporate fresh or revised course content, staying aligned with evolving educational objectives.
Improved Feedback
The goal is to provide timely, context-rich feedback that supports holistic learning across diverse modes of student expression.

Rationale

In the proposed system, instead of solely relying on its built-in parameters, the LLM uses knowledge retrieval mechanisms to ensure its assessment is consistent, knowledge-rich, and accurate based on the current learning resources. It integrates external resources dynamically and delivers personalized and well-informed feedback aligned with the specific mode of the student's expression.

RAG then harmonizes these nuanced evaluation insights, enabling the LLM to provide authentic appraisals aligned with broader pedagogical objectives and subject-specific standards. By using this method, the multimodal auto-grading system can be made scalable, adaptable, and precise, thanks to the continuous learning potential of the RAG and the nuanced evaluation of the LLM.

Research Methodology

To build a robust dataset for the RAG approach, various academic materials such as lecture slides, textbooks, assignment prompts, and grading rubrics will be compiled in digital form. Additionally, anonymized student submissions will be incorporated to capture the diversity of real-world responses.

In the proposed multimodal auto-grading system, the LLM is enhanced by the RAG, a data reinforcement learning model. This integration allows the LLM to combine its expertise with external knowledge from databases, improving the evaluation process.

Year 1: Literature Review & Foundation
Focus on finding and making a systematic literature review on the subject of Large Language Models, Retrieval-Augmented Generation, auto-grading systems, and other relevant papers and publications. Focus more on the implementation, rather than the algorithm.
Year 2: Prototype Development
Develop a prototype of the proposed system focused mainly on its functionality. The prototype will be developed using available resources gained using given funds.
Year 3: System Completion & Testing
Complete the prototype development and spend the rest of the year debugging the system and making sure that it's ready to be used.
Year 4: Thesis & Defense
Focus on building the thesis and answering the research problem. The prototype will serve as a testing tool. First half for testing, second half for compiling and publishing findings.

Benefits

Theoretical Advancement
Expands understanding of how AI can handle diverse inputs within a single framework, challenging current NLP-based approaches.
Educational Equality
Promotes education equality by reducing grading burden and ensuring all students receive timely, detailed feedback regardless of submission format.
Innovation Impact
Outlines a novel multimodal autograding approach that merges LLM with RAG to effectively evaluate students' work across multiple media types.
